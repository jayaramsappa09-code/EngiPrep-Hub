01

Introduction & Coherence Dynamics

Solids are classified into Crystalline and Amorphous states depending on their atomic arrangement. In a crystal, constituents are bound by high-cohesion electrostatic forces forming regular, periodic structural planes.

Dimensional Coherence & Spatial Order:

• Long-Range Order: Periodicity extends over thousands of lattice parameters. This spatial coherence ensures constructive interference when interacting with electromagnetic fields.

• Crystal Cohesion: Arises from the minimization of Gibbs free energy when atoms align perfectly into energy-well positions of the periodic space lattice.

To study this periodic state mathematically, we define the following relationship:

Lattice + Basis = Crystal Structure
Coherent Vector Concept

Coherent translation of the basis unit yields the physical crystalline matter.

Key Core Principles of Crystallography

Fundamental Unit

Defining the Unit Cell

The Unit Cell is defined as the smallest structural volume or building block of a crystal, which, when translated repeatedly in three directions, generates the complete crystal lattice macrostructure.

Lattice Parameters (The 6 Configuration Constants):

The size, geometry, and spatial orientation of a unit cell are uniquely defined by six scalar variables:

  • Three axial lengths (interceptss): $a, b, c$ along the $x, y, z$ crystallographic axes.
  • Three axial angles:
    • • $\alpha$: angle between directions $b$ and $c$ (on y-z plane)
    • • $\beta$: angle between directions $a$ and $c$ (on x-z plane)
    • • $\gamma$: angle between directions $a$ and $b$ (on x-y plane)

03

Essential Crystallography Definitions

Definitions you MUST write precisely in the JNTUK examination to fetch high marks:

1. Space Lattice

An infinite, periodic three-dimensional arrangement of imaginary points in space where each point has an identical neighborhood.

2. Basis (Motif)

A group of one or more atoms attached identically to each and every lattice point to manifest physical crystal structures.

3. Conventional vs Primitive Cell

• Primitive Cell: Smallest unit cell having lattice points ONLY at the corners (Effective atoms $N=1$).
• Conventional Cell: Crystal unit containing multiple lattice points representing complete symmetry (e.g. BCC, FCC).

4. Coordination Number (CN)

The number of nearest-neighbor atomic spheres that directly touch any reference atomic sphere in the crystal structure.

5. Atomic Radius (r)

Half of the nearest interatomic separation distance $d$ between two co-planar identical touching atoms in a stable lattice.

6. Atomic Packing Fraction (APF)

The ratio of total volumetric space occupied by hard atomic spheres inside a unit cell to the total geometric space volume of the unit cell itself.

7. Miller Indices (h k l)

The set of three smallest integers derived as inverses of intercepts of a crystallographic plane along axial coordinates, cleared of fractional multipliers.

04

Core Board Derivations (APF & Spacing)

For cubic crystals, we analyze three primary types of structures: Simple Cubic (SC), Body-Centered Cubic (BCC), and Face-Centered Cubic (FCC). Memorize these five specific key properties for each system:

Simple Cubic (SC)

Single Corner Atoms

  • • Coordination Number (CN): 6
  • • Effective atoms per unit cell (N): 1
    (8 corner atoms * 1/8 each)
  • • Relation (Radius r vs Axis a):
    r = a / 2
  • • Nearest Neighbor Distance (d): a
  • • Atomic Packing Factor (APF): 0.52 (52.3%)
Examples: Polonium (Po)
Body-Centered Cubic (BCC)

Central Core Atom Included

  • • Coordination Number (CN): 8
  • • Effective atoms per unit cell (N): 2
    (8 corners * 1/8 + 1 inside center)
  • • Relation (Radius r vs Axis a):
    r = (R √3) / 4
  • • Nearest Neighbor Distance (d): a√3 / 2
  • • Atomic Packing Factor (APF): 0.68 (68%)
Examples: Iron (Fe), Sodium (Na), W
Face-Centered Cubic (FCC)

Shared Facet Plates Atoms

  • • Coordination Number (CN): 12
  • • Effective atoms per unit cell (N): 4
    (8 corners * 1/8 + 6 faces * 1/2 each)
  • • Relation (Radius r vs Axis a):
    r = a / (2√2)
  • • Nearest Neighbor Distance (d): a / √2
  • • Atomic Packing Factor (APF): 0.74 (74%) (Tightest)
Examples: Copper (Cu), Gold (Au), Al

High-Yield Formula Sheet

The absolute quickest cheat-sheet to write during your 10-minute revision before entering the exam room. Memorize these relations:

1. Crystal Lattice Relationship
Lattice Points + Basis = Crystal Structure
No crystal sits in space without the basis motif attached to points.
2. Interplanar Spacing $d_{hkl}$ (Orthogonal)
$$d_{hkl} = \frac{a}{\sqrt{h^2 + k^2 + l^2}}$$
Where h, k, l are Miller Indices and a is the lattice parameter.
3. Metallic Cubic Key Constants Quick Table
Structure Type Coordination No. (CN) Atomic Radius (r) Packing Fraction (APF) Volume ($V_{\text{cell}}$)
Simple Cubic (SC) 6 r = a / 2 0.52 (52.3%) a³ = 8r³
Body-Centered (BCC) 8 r = a√3 / 4 0.68 (68.0%) a³ = (4r/√3)³
Face-Centered (FCC) 12 r = a / (2√2) 0.74 (74.1%) a³ = (2r√2)³
4. Volumetric Density of Crystal ($\rho$)
$$\rho = \frac{N \times M}{V_{\text{cell}} \times N_A} = \frac{N \times M}{a^3 \times N_A}$$
Where N = effective atoms, M = atomic weight, a = cell edge key, $N_A = \text{Avogadro's Number} = 6.023 \times 10^{23}$.

Standard Solved Board Numericals

Problem 1: Interplanar spacing JNTUK Repeat 🎯

Question: Calculate the interplanar d-spacing for planes represented as (1 0 0), (1 1 0), and (1 1 1) inside a simple cubic crystal cell carrying lattice parameter a = 4.2 Å.

Standard step-by-step solution:

1. Formula: $d_{hkl} = a / \sqrt{h^2 + k^2 + l^2}$. Here $a = 4.2 \text{ \AA}$.

2. For (1 0 0) plane: $d_{100} = 4.2 / \sqrt{1^2 + 0^2 + 0^2} = 4.2 / 1 = \mathbf{4.2 \text{ \AA}}$.

3. For (1 1 0) plane: $d_{110} = 4.2 / \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2 + 0^2} = 4.2 / \sqrt{2} \approx \mathbf{2.97 \text{ \AA}}$.

4. For (1 1 1) plane: $d_{111} = 4.2 / \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2 + 1^2} = 4.2 / \sqrt{3} \approx \mathbf{2.42 \text{ \AA}}$.

Problem 2: Density Computation 10-Marks Standard

Question: A crystal solid has metallic BCC structure with atomic radius R = 1.24 Å. Find the theoretical density if its atomic weight M = 55.85 g/mol.

Standard step-by-step solution:

1. Under BCC structure: Effective atoms per unit cell, $N = 2$.

2. Find Lattice constantly a: $r = a\sqrt{3}/4 \Rightarrow a = 4r / \sqrt{3}$.

    $a = (4 \times 1.24 \times 10^{-8} \text{ cm}) / 1.732 = 2.863 \times 10^{-8} \text{ cm}$.

3. Solve Unit cell Volume: $V_{cell} = a^3 = (2.863 \times 10^{-8})^3 = 2.347 \times 10^{-23} \text{ cm}^3$.

4. Evaluate Density equation: $\rho = (N \times M) / (N_A \times V_{cell})$

    $\rho = (2 \times 55.85) / [6.022 \times 10^{23} \times 2.347 \times 10^{-23}]$

    $\rho = 111.7 / 14.133 \approx \mathbf{7.90 \text{ g/cm}^3}$

Direct archive of actual questions asked in recent JNTUK semester examinations. Study the official marking schemes and expert answers to maximize your score:

JNTUK R23 Apr/May 2024 Regular 10 Marks Solved ✅

Question: Deduce the relation between plane spacing (d) and lattice parameter (a) for cubic lattice planes with Miller indices (h k l). Also, find the ratio of d₁₀₀ : d₁₁₀ : d₁₁₁ for a cubic structure.

Part A: Derivation of $d_{hkl}$ (7 Marks)

• Establish Cartesian axes with origin O. If the next parallel plane cuts intercepts on x, y, and z axes at $x_1, y_1, z_1$, then: x₁ = a/h, y₁ = a/k, z₁ = a/l.

• Perpendicular distance $d = OP$. Let normal make angles $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ with axes.

• Projections yield direction cosines: cos(α) = dh/a, cos(β) = dk/a, cos(γ) = dl/a.

• Since $\cos^2\alpha + \cos^2\beta + \cos^2\gamma = 1$, substituting ratios gives:
(d² / a²) * (h² + k² + l²) = 1 ⟹ d = a / √(h² + k² + l²)

Part B: Ratio Calculation (3 Marks)

• $d_{100} = a / \sqrt{1} = a$

• $d_{110} = a / \sqrt{2}$

• $d_{111} = a / \sqrt{3}$

Ratio: $d_{100} : d_{110} : d_{111} = a : \frac{a}{\sqrt{2}} : \frac{a}{\sqrt{3}} = 1 : \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} : \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \approx \mathbf{1 : 0.707 : 0.577}$.

JNTUK R20 Nov/Dec 2023 Regular 10 Marks Solved ✅

Question: Distinguish between Space Lattice and Unit cell. Derive coordination number and atomic packing fraction of BCC and FCC crystal structures.

Part A: Distinctions (2 Marks)

• **Space Lattice**: Infinite, periodic spatial array of mathematical coordinate points in 3D space.

• **Unit Cell**: Smallest structural volume unit of the lattice which, when repeated in 3 directions, forms the entire block crystal.

Part B: BCC Derivation (4 Marks)

• Effective number of atoms: $N = 2$. Coordination number: $CN = 8$.

• Relation: $\sqrt{3}a = 4r \Rightarrow r = a\sqrt{3}/4$.

• Processing packing equation: $\text{APF} = \frac{2 \times (4/3)\pi r^3}{a^3} = \frac{\sqrt{3}\pi}{8} \approx \mathbf{0.680 \ (68\%)}$.

Part C: FCC Derivation (4 Marks)

• Effective number of atoms: $N = 4$. Coordination number: $CN = 12$.

• Relation: $\sqrt{2}a = 4r \Rightarrow r = a / 2\sqrt{2}$.

• Processing packing equation: $\text{APF} = \frac{4 \times (4/3)\pi r^3}{a^3} = \frac{\pi}{3\sqrt{2}} \approx \mathbf{0.741 \ (74\%)}$.

01. Symmetry Parameters FLIP 🧪

Why is Triclinic system known as the least symmetric?

Tap to review answer

Because in Triclinic lattices, none of the parameters align: $a \neq b \neq c$ and none of the angles match mutually ($\alpha \neq \beta \neq \gamma \neq 90^\circ$). This yields zero spatial coordinate symmetries.

Awarded +10 XP
02. Lattice Constant FLIP 🧪

What does coordination number represent physically in packing?

Tap to review answer

It physically counts the total number of nearest neighbor atoms directly touching any reference atom inside the cubic grid system. It represents grid coordination density. (SC=6, BCC=8, FCC=12)

Awarded +10 XP

Crystalline vs Amorphous Quick Sheet

1. Crystalline Solids

  • • Regular, periodic 3D arrangement of atoms.
  • • True solids with sharp, definite melting points.
  • • Anisotropic (physical properties vary along axes).
  • • Long-range order of atoms is maintained.

2. Amorphous Solids

  • • Random, disordered, non-periodic arrangement.
  • • Pseudo-solids (supercooled liquids) with melting range.
  • • Isotropic (identical properties in all directions).
  • • Short-range local order only.

1-Day Prep Final Tips (Board Exam emergency checklist)

Save failing scores with 3 instant rules:

Rule 1:

Never skip APF derivations. It represents 10 marks of solid physical attributes. Practice BCC geometry corner and face chords proof three times today.

Rule 2:

Draw coordinates systems axes when writing hkl intercepts on sheets. Intercepts parallel to an axis go to ∞ so index is 0. Mention variables explicitly.

Rule 3:

State axial constant rules clearly. For instance, a basic Monoclinic axis relation is highly specific ($a \neq b \neq c$, $\beta \neq 90^\circ$). Paper assessors give instant marks for exact formulas.

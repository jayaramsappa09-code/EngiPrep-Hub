1. Unit Overview & Exam Weight

This unit transitions classical engineering from deterministic Newtonian motion into probabilistic quantum mechanics and free electron behavior in metals. The core grading requirements of JNTUK in this chapter focus enormously on setting up bounds for 1D Infinite Potential Wells and deriving its energy eigenvalues.

Exam Importance

One massive Part B question containing the Schrödinger 1D box proof (10 Marks) is guaranteed to repeat.

Practical Relevance

Quantum tunneling and energy eigenvalue limits dictate flash memory thresholds and scanning tunneling microscopes (STM).

2. Complete Syllabus Core Concepts

Concept 01

de Broglie Dual Nature

Hypothesis: Moving particles like electrons possess associated dual-nature wave properties called pilot/matter waves.

\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mE_K}}

Exams Warning: Never relate matter waves to electromagnetic field waves. Matter waves have no charge requirements and depend purely on momentum!

Concept 02

Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

Definition: It is physically impossible to determine both position coordinate ($x$) and momentum vector ($p$) of a micro-particle simultaneously with infinite precision.

\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2} \quad \left(\hbar = \frac{h}{2\pi}\right)

Exam application: Explains why an electron can never reside inside the nucleus (as its velocity would exceed speed of light).

Concept 03

Wave Function Properties & Significance

Definition: A complex variable amplitude $\Psi(x, y, z, t)$ containing the complete state description of a quantum particle. Max Born's rule states that the probability density is $|\Psi|^2 = \Psi \Psi^*$.

Born Requirements: Wave function must be finite, continuous, single-valued, and normalizable.

Concept 04

Schrödinger Wave Equations

Definition: Fundamental wave equations describing quantum particle state shifts under external potentials $V$.

  • Time-Independent: $\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + \frac{8\pi^2m}{h^2}(E - V)\psi = 0$
  • Time-Dependent: $i\hbar\frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2 \Psi}{\partial x^2} + V\Psi$
Concept 05

1D Infinite Potential Well

Concept: A particle trapped in a line segment $x \in [0, L]$ with $V(x) = 0$ inside and $V(x) = \infty$ outside. The boundary barriers force the particle to take discrete, quantized energy levels.

E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8 m L^2} \quad \text{and} \quad \psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)
Concept 06

Classical Free Electron Theory

Drude-Lorentz Model: Approximates valence electrons as a mobile gas bounded by metallic positive ion cores under classical kinetic laws.

  • Drift Velocity: Velocity gained under external electric field ($v_d = \mu E$).
  • Mean Free Path ($\lambda_c$): Avg distance traveled between two consecutive collisions.
  • Relaxation Time ($\tau$): Time taken for drift velocity to reduce to $1/e$ when the field is turned off.
Concept 07

Merits & Demerits of Classical Theory

Critique: Classical theory succeeded in proving Ohm's law and Widemann-Franz law, but failed fundamentally on electronic properties.

• Failure 1: Predicts electronic specific heat proportional to $T$, while actual is $100\times$ smaller.
• Failure 2: Predicts electrical conductivity $\sigma \propto 1/\sqrt{T}$, whereas experimental is $\sigma \propto 1/T$.

Concept 08

Quantum Free Electron Theory

Sommerfeld Model: Solved electronic anomalies by introducing Fermi-Dirac statistics and restricting electrons to Pauli exclusion states inside a 3D potential box.

Only conduction electrons near the Fermi boundary $E_F$ can absorb heat and drift under applied fields!

Concept 09

Fermi-Dirac Distribution & Fermi Level

Definition: Specifies the state probability function $f(E)$ representing the likelihood of electron occupancy at energy $E$ and temperature $T$.

f(E) = \frac{1}{e^{\frac{E-E_F}{kT}} + 1}

At absolute zero ($T = 0 \text{ K}$), $f(E) = 1$ for all $E \le E_F$ and $f(E) = 0$ for all $E > E_F$.

Concept 10

Density of States & Fermi Energy

Definition: Density of states $Z(E)dE$ denotes the number of allowed quantum electron states per unit volume between energies $E$ and $E+dE$. Fermi energy is the highest quantum state energy level at $T=0\text{ K}$.

E_F(0) = \frac{h^2}{8m} \left( \frac{3n}{\pi} \right)^{2/3}

where $n$ is conduction electron concentration per unit volume.

⚡ 3. High-Yield Formula Sheet & Memory Aids

1. de Broglie Wavelength Variations

  • Using Kinetic Energy E: \lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2mE}}
  • For accelerated electrons: \lambda = \frac{12.27}{\sqrt{V}} \text{ Å}

2. Schrödinger Time-Independent 1D

\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + \frac{8\pi^2m}{h^2}(E - V)\psi = 0

Relates spatial wave function amplitude to total energy E and potential coordinates V.

3. 1D Box Quantum Energy Level

E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8 m L^2}

n represents positive non-zero integers (1, 2, 3...). Zero-point energy (n=1) is $E_1 = \frac{h^2}{8mL^2}$.

4. Density of States Z(E) dE

Z(E) dE = \frac{4\pi}{h^3} (2m)^{3/2} E^{1/2} dE

Gives total allowed electron states per unit volume in the energy interval $E$ and $E+dE$.

5. Fermi-Dirac Distribution

f(E) = \frac{1}{e^{\frac{E - E_F}{kT}} + 1}

Probability function. At $T > 0 \text{ K}$, $f(E) = 0.5$ when $E = E_F$ — the definition of Fermi Level at any temperature.

6. Quantum Electrical Conductivity

\sigma_q = \frac{n e^2 \tau_F}{m^*}

Quantum formulation based on relaxation time $\tau_F$ at Fermi energy surface levels and effective electron mass $m^*$.

7. Fermi Energy Formula (0 K)

E_F = \frac{h^2}{8m} \left(\frac{3n}{\pi}\right)^{2/3}

Depends solely on conduction electron density $n$, proving $E_F$ is constant for a given metal.

8. Average Energy (Quantum vs Classical)

E_{avg} = \frac{3}{5} E_F \quad [T = 0 \text{ K}]

In stark contrast to classical kinetic theory predicting zero average energy at $T=0\text{ K}$!

📝 4. Step-by-Step Board Derivations

JNTUK Elite Core Proof 1

1. Particle inside 1D Infinite Potential Box

Let a quantum particle be bound inside a 1-dimensional box of width $L$ with boundaries $x=0$ and $x=L$. Within boundaries, potential energy is $V = 0$. Outside boundaries, $V = \infty$.

Step 1: Setup Schrödinger Boundary Conditions
Inside the box ($V=0$), the 1D Schrödinger equation is:
$\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + \frac{8\pi^2mE}{h^2}\psi = 0 \implies \frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + k^2\psi = 0 \quad \left[\text{where } k^2 = \frac{8\pi^2mE}{h^2}\right]$

Step 2: General Solution Mapping
The general spatial wave solution is:
$\psi(x) = A \sin(kx) + B \cos(kx)$

Step 3: Impose Boundary Limits Coordinates
• At boundary 1 ($x=0$): $\psi(0) = B \cos(0) = 0 \implies B = 0$.
• At boundary 2 ($x=L$): $\psi(L) = A \sin(kL) = 0$.
Since $A \neq 0$ (otherwise particle does not exist), we must have:
$\sin(kL) = 0 \implies kL = n\pi \implies k = \frac{n\pi}{L} \quad (\text{for } n = 1, 2, 3...)$

Step 4: Energy Eigenvalues Deduction (Boxed Answer)
Equate the two definitions of $k^2$:
$\left(\frac{n\pi}{L}\right)^2 = \frac{8\pi^2mE_n}{h^2} \implies E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8 m L^2}$.

Step 5: Normalize Wavefunction to Unity Limits
$\int_0^L |\psi(x)|^2 dx = 1 \implies A^2 \int_0^L \sin^2\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) dx = 1 \implies A = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}$.
Final normalized wavefunction is $\psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}} \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)$.

JNTUK Elite Core Proof 2

2. Schrödinger's Time-Independent Wave Equation

Derives wave behavioral changes relative to space coordinates under steady potentials (energy does not change over time).

Step 1: Classical de Broglie wave equation
Let a particle of mass $m$ be moving with a velocity $v$ along the $x$-axis. The harmonic wave equation associated with the particle is:
$\psi(x, t) = \psi(x) e^{-i\omega t} \quad \text{where } \psi(x) = A e^{ikx}$

Step 2: Differentiating relative to spatial coordinate $x$ twice
$\frac{d\psi}{dx} = ik A e^{ikx} \implies \frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} = (ik)^2 A e^{ikx} = -k^2 \psi(x) \quad [i^2 = -1]$

Step 3: Substitute the de Broglie wavelength definition
We know that $k = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$ and $\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$ ⇒ $k^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{\lambda^2} = \frac{4\pi^2 p^2}{h^2}$.
Plugging this into the spatial derivative:
$\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + \frac{4\pi^2 p^2}{h^2}\psi(x) = 0$

Step 4: Relate Momentum to Kinetic and Potential Energy
Total Energy $E = K.E. + P.E. \implies E = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V \implies p^2 = 2m(E - V)$.
Substitute $p^2$ back into our equation:
$\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + \frac{8\pi^2m}{h^2}(E - V)\psi = 0$ (This is the Time-Independent Schrödinger equation).

JNTUK Elite Core Proof 3

3. Schrödinger's Time-Dependent Wave Equation

Builds a complete wave model where boundary conditions or states are transient and evolve over time.

Step 1: Start with time-varying wave segment
Consider the complete wave function $\Psi(x, t) = \psi_0 e^{i(kx - \omega t)}$ representing a moving particle.

Step 2: Differentiate with respect to time $t$ once
$\frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = -i \omega \psi_0 e^{i(kx - \omega t)} = -i \omega \Psi$
Since energy $E = h\nu = \hbar \omega$ (where $\omega = 2\pi\nu$), substitute $\omega = \frac{E}{\hbar}$:
$\frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = -i \frac{E}{\hbar} \Psi \implies E\Psi = -\frac{\hbar}{i} \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = i \hbar \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} \quad \left[\text{since } \frac{1}{i} = -i\right]$

Step 3: Integrate with spatial dimensions
Using Schrödinger's Time-Independent version: $-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + V\psi = E\psi$.
Substitute the time operator $E\Psi = i\hbar\frac{\partial\Psi}{\partial t}$ directly into the total mechanical energy balance of the wave function:
$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2\Psi(x,t)}{\partial x^2} + V\Psi(x,t) = i\hbar\frac{\partial\Psi(x,t)}{\partial t}$.

JNTUK Elite Core Proof 4

4. Electrical Conductivity Based on Quantum Free Electron Theory

Sommerfeld's rigorous proof relating electrical conduction properties to electron distributions restricted strictly near the Fermi level.

Step 1: Understand why Classical theory failed
In classical theory, ALL valence electrons are assumed to participate in conduction. In quantum theory, only electrons residing near the Fermi energy level $E_F$ can gain energy and assist inside conduction paths.

Step 2: Relate current density to drifting electron gas
Current density $J = n e v_d$, where $v_d$ is the drift velocity of active electrons at the Fermi surface.
According to Quantum statistics, $v_d = \frac{e E \tau_F}{m^*}$, where $\tau_F$ is the relaxation time at the Fermi energy levels, and $m^*$ is the effective mass of the electron.

Step 3: Combine with Ohm's law
$J = n e \left( \frac{e E \tau_F}{m^*} \right) \implies J = \left( \frac{n e^2 \tau_F}{m^*} \right) E$.
Since Ohm's law states $J = \sigma_q E$, comparing coefficients yields:
$\sigma_q = \frac{n e^2 \tau_F}{m^*}$.

6. Fully Solved JNTUK Numerical Models

Problem Model 1

Problem: Find the kinetic energy of an electron accelerated from rest acting under potential drop V = 150 Volts. Express its de Broglie matter pilot wavelength.

Solution steps:
1. Direct Accelerated Equation: $\lambda = 12.27 / \sqrt{V} \text{ Å}$.
2. Substitute parameters values: $\lambda = 12.27 / \sqrt{150} = 12.27 / 12.247 = 1.002\text{ Å}$.
Final Output: Wavelength lambda = 1.002 Å
Problem Model 2

Problem: Calculate the zero-point energy ($n=1$) and the first excited state energy ($n=2$) of an electron inside an infinite 1D box of width $L = 2 \text{ Å}$.

Solution steps:
1. Ground state energy formula: $E_1 = \frac{h^2}{8mL^2}$.
Given constants: $h = 6.626\times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$, $m = 9.109\times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$, $L = 2\times 10^{-10} \text{ m}$.
2. Calculate: $E_1 = \frac{(6.626\times 10^{-34})^2}{8 \times 9.109\times 10^{-31} \times (2\times 10^{-10})^2} = 1.506 \times 10^{-18} \text{ Joules}$.
3. Convert to eV: $E_1 = \frac{1.506\times 10^{-18}}{1.602\times 10^{-19}} = 9.40 \text{ eV}$.
4. First excited state ($n=2$): $E_2 = 2^2 \times E_1 = 4 \times 9.40 \text{ eV} = 37.6 \text{ eV}$.
Final Outputs: E_1 = 9.40 eV & E_2 = 37.6 eV
Problem Model 3

Problem: Find the Fermi energy of copper at $0 \text{ K}$, assuming its conduction electron density is $n = 8.49 \times 10^{28} \text{ m}^{-3}$.

Solution steps:
1. Use the $T = 0 \text{ K}$ Fermi Energy expression:
$E_F = \frac{h^2}{8m} \left( \frac{3n}{\pi} \right)^{2/3}$.
2. Substitute parameters: $\frac{h^2}{8m} = 6.02 \times 10^{-38} \text{ J}$. And $\left(\frac{3 \times 8.49\times 10^{28}}{3.1416}\right)^{2/3} = 1.87 \times 10^{19} \text{ m}^{-2}$.
3. Energy $E_F = 1.124 \times 10^{-18} \text{ J} = 7.02 \text{ eV}$.
Final Output: Fermi level E_f = 7.02 eV

📋 JNTUK Unit IV Solved Papers Highly Repeated Solutions

JNTUK Nov 2024 / R23 10 Marks

State Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle and prove that free electrons cannot reside inside the nucleus.

Statement: The product of uncertainty in position ($\Delta x$) and uncertainty in momentum ($\Delta p$) is always greater than or equal to $\hbar/2$.

Nuclear Electron Impossibility Proof: Let the diameter of an atomic nucleus be $10^{-14} \text{ m}$. Thus, the maximum uncertainty in the electron's position inside the nucleus is $\Delta x \approx 10^{-14} \text{ m}$.

By Heisenberg principle: $\Delta p \ge \frac{h}{4\pi \Delta x} = \frac{6.626\times 10^{-34}}{4 \cdot \pi \cdot 10^{-14}} = 5.27 \times 10^{-21} \text{ kg}\cdot\text{m/s}$.

Assuming the particle momentum is around this uncertainty: $p \approx \Delta p$, its corresponding kinetic energy is:
$E_K = \frac{p^2}{2m} = \frac{(5.27 \times 10^{-21})^2}{2 \times 9.1 \times 10^{-31}} = 1.53 \times 10^{-11} \text{ J} \approx 95 \text{ MeV}$.

However, beta decay emissions show that electrons emitted from nuclei have at most $3\text{--}4 \text{ MeV}$ energy. Since $95 \text{ MeV} \gg 4 \text{ MeV}$, free electrons can never survive inside atomic nuclei bounds!

JNTUK May 2023 8 Marks

Write down the general assumptions and core deficiencies of Drude-Lorentz Classical Free Electron theory of metals.

• Assumptions: Metals consist of ion cores with free valency electrons wandering randomly. Electrons behave like neutral gas molecules obeying classical Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics. Mutual repulsion between electrons is completely ignored.

• Deficiencies: (1) Predicted electronic specific heat $C_v = \frac{3}{2}R$, whereas real observed value is $\sim 100\times$ lower ($0.015 R$). (2) Predicted Wiedemann-Franz ratio is only approximately correct, failing dramatically at low temperatures. (3) Classical theory failed to explain why some metals are superconductors.

9. Concept Viva Flashcards

Click any card below to flip and inspect the rapid exam answers preferred by external lab examiners.

Q1. Can the quantum zero-point energy of a particle inside a box be zero?

Flip card to read answer 🔄

No. Minimum energy (zero-point energy for $n=1$) lies above zero. If energy drops to zero, the momentum goes to zero, which violently violates Heisenberg uncertainty!

Solved Concept Answer

Q2. What is the physical significance of Fermi energy levels?

Flip card to read answer 🔄

At $T = 0 \text{ K}$, it acts as the exact boundary separating occupied states from empty states. At temperatures $T > 0 \text{ K}$, it represents the state with an exact 50% chance of occupation.

Solved Concept Answer

Q3. Why does de Broglie's wave character only show up in subatomic particles?

Flip card to read answer 🔄

Since wavelength is inversely proportional to mass ($\lambda = h/mv$), macroscopic bodies have huge masses making the wave-amplitude virtually immeasurable.

Solved Concept Answer

Q4. What physical property does the density of states measure?

Flip card to read answer 🔄

It measures the crowding of kinetic energy levels inside bulk metals. A high density of states suggests that there are many available energy slots in an interval.

Solved Concept Answer

Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

Wave function properties mnemonic

Remembering Born's 4 Wave conditions

Use the phrase "S-C-U-F (Smart Cats Understand Physics)" representing:
• Single-valued: Particle can have only one amplitude at any coordinate.
• Continuous: Boundary curves must be smooth without physical breaks.
• Unitary / Normalized: Total likelihood of finding particle overall space is 1.
• Finite: Energy levels of real physical systems cannot diverge to infinity inside systems.

Energy Level Index Trick

Quantum Energy E_n shortcut

Think of potential box eigenvalues as "n-squared scale":
• $E_2$ is 4x ground state ($E_1$).
• $E_3$ is 9x ground state ($E_1$).
• $E_4$ is 16x ground state ($E_1$).
Pro-Tip: Zero state ($n=0$) is strictly forbidden by uncertainty. Zero occupancy violates minimum entropy boundaries!

1. Unit Overview & Exam Strategy

Under the JNTUK R23 syllabus, Unit I of Mathematics-I covers ordinary differential equations of first order and first degree. This is a highly algorithmic unit – once you learn to properly identify the form of an equation, solving it is a guaranteed process. It establishes the mathematical basis for transient models inside physical, chemical, and electrical systems.

Exam Blueprint Analysis

JNTUK typically asks two long questions from this unit: one pure mathematical system (usually Bernoulli's reduction or Exact/Reducible to Exact) and one practical modeling derivation (mostly a Newton's Law of Cooling cooling schedule or an LR Circuit transient current equation). Doing both perfectly guarantees 14-16 marks!

Engineering Applications

This math models current growth in inductors, voltage discharge dynamics inside capacitor filters, temperature leakage inside boiler systems, and nuclear fuel degradation tracking.

2. JNTUK Syllabus Core Concepts

Topper-curated Definitions
01
Type 1: Linear

Linear Differential Equations

Definition: A differential equation is linear if the dependent variable $y$ and its derivative $dy/dx$ appear only in the first degree and are not multiplied together.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x) y = Q(x)$$

Where $P$ and $Q$ are continuous functions of $x$ only. Solution is achieved using an **Integrating Factor** $I.F. = e^{\int P dx}$.

  • Examiner Trap: Ensure the coefficient of $dy/dx$ is exactly $1$ before identifying $P(x)$ and $Q(x)$. Divide through first!
  • Formula Variant: If linear in $x$, the form is $\frac{dx}{dy} + P(y) x = Q(y)$, with $I.F. = e^{\int P(y) dy}$.
02
Type 2: Reducible to Linear

Bernoulli's Equations

Definition: An equation of the first order and first degree that is non-linear but can be readily reduced to linear form through a variable substitution.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x) y = Q(x) y^n \quad (n \neq 0, 1)$$

Solved by dividing through by $y^n$, resulting in $y^{-n}\frac{dy}{dx} + Py^{1-n} = Q$. Substituting $v = y^{1-n}$ transforms it to a standard linear form in $v$.

  • Exam Tip: Be careful with negative indices. If $n = -2$, write the substitution variable $v = y^{1 - (-2)} = y^3$.
03
Type 3: Exactness

Exact Differential Equations

Definition: A differential equation of the form $M(x,y)dx + N(x,y)dy = 0$ is exact if its left-hand side matches the exact total differential $du$ of some function $u(x,y)$.

$$\text{Necessary and Sufficient Condition: } \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$$

Solution Formula: $\int M dx$ (keeping $y$ constant) $+ \int (\text{terms in } N \text{ free from } x) dy = C$.

  • Topper Tip: "Free from $x$" means terms that do not contain $x$ at all. For example, in $N = \sin x + y^2$, the term free from $x$ is just $y^2$. If $N = xy$, there are no terms free from $x$!
04
Type 4: Reducible to Exact

Equations Reducible to Exact

Definition: If $Mdx + Ndy = 0$ is not exact ($\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \neq \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$), we multiply by an Integrating Factor ($I.F.$) to make it exact.

Four Core JNTUK Rules:
1. **Homogeneous**: If $Mx+Ny \neq 0$, then $I.F. = \frac{1}{Mx+Ny}$.
2. **Form $y f(xy)dx + x g(xy)dy = 0$**: If $Mx-Ny \neq 0$, $I.F. = \frac{1}{Mx-Ny}$.
3. **Function of $x$**: If $\frac{1}{N}\left(\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}\right) = f(x)$, then $I.F. = e^{\int f(x) dx}$.
4. **Function of $y$**: If $\frac{1}{M}\left(\frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y}\right) = g(y)$, then $I.F. = e^{\int g(y) dy}$.

05
Application Topic A

Newton's Cooling & Growth-Decay

Newton's Law of Cooling: The time rate of change of temperature $T(t)$ of a body is proportional to the difference between its temperature and the ambient medium temperature $T_s$.

$$\frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_s) \implies T(t) = T_s + (T_0 - T_s)e^{-kt}$$

Natural Growth/Decay: Rate of increase of a quantity $N$ is proportional to its current quantity: $\frac{dN}{dt} = \pm k N \implies N(t) = N_0 e^{\pm kt}$.

06
Application Topic B

Electrical LRC circuit dynamics

L-R Series Circuit: By Kirchhoff's Voltage Law, the sum of inductive voltage drop $L(di/dt)$ and resistive drop $Ri$ must equal applied source electromotive force $E(t)$. This models a linear first-order DE in transient current $i(t)$:

$$L \frac{di}{dt} + R i = E(t) \implies \frac{di}{dt} + \frac{R}{L} i = \frac{E(t)}{L}$$

R-C Series Circuit: Modeled in terms of capacitor charge accumulation $q(t)$:

$$R \frac{dq}{dt} + \frac{q}{C} = E(t) \implies \frac{dq}{dt} + \frac{1}{RC} q = \frac{E(t)}{R}$$

⚡ 3. High-Yield Formula Sheet & Memory Aids

1. Linear DE Solution ($dy/dx + Py = Q$)

$$I.F. = e^{\int P(x) dx}$$ $$y \cdot (I.F.) = \int Q(x) \cdot (I.F.) dx + C$$

• $P(x)$ and $Q(x)$ must be functions of $x$ (or constants) only.
• Tip: Remember $\int \tan x dx = \ln(\sec x)$, giving $e^{\int \tan x dx} = \sec x$.

2. Bernoulli Transformation

$$\frac{dy}{dx} + Py = Q y^n \implies \frac{dv}{dx} + (1-n)Pv = (1-n)Q$$

• Made linear using substitution: $v = y^{1-n}$.
• The linear integrating factor becomes $I.F. = e^{(1-n)\int P dx}$.

3. Exact Equation Solver

$$\int_{(y=\text{const})} M dx + \int_{\text{(terms free from } x\text{)}} N dy = C$$

• Verify $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$ first.
• Tip: Do NOT integrate terms of $N$ containing products of $x$, e.g., if $N = x^2 y + y^3$, only integrate $y^3$.

4. Integrating Factors (Reducible Form)

$$\text{If Homogeneous: } I.F. = \frac{1}{Mx+Ny}$$ $$\text{If } \frac{1}{N}\left(\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}\right) = f(x) \implies I.F. = e^{\int f(x)dx}$$ $$\text{If } \frac{1}{M}\left(\frac{\partial N}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial M}{\partial y}\right) = g(y) \implies I.F. = e^{\int g(y)dy}$$

• Apply sequentially: check Homogeneity, then try function of $x$, then function of $y$.

5. Newton's Cooling Formulation

$$\ln(T - T_s) = -k t + \ln(T_0 - T_s)$$ $$k = \frac{1}{t} \ln\left(\frac{T_0 - T_s}{T_t - T_s}\right)$$

• $T_0$: Initial body temperature (at $t = 0$).
• $T_s$: Surrounding room temperature constant.
• $T_t$: Body temperature at time $t$.

Mnemonic: Integrating Factors

"My Minus Nx divides through N!"

Think of Rule 3: The numerator is always $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$ ("My minus Nx"). If we divide by $N$, we get a function of $x$ (so it matches $N$ having $x$ columns). If we divide by $-M$, we get a function of $y$!

📝 4. Step-by-Step Academically Valid Derivations

JNTUK 10 Marks Derivation 01

How Integrating Factor (I.F.) Solves Linear First-Order Equations

Step-by-step rigorous development of why multiplying by $e^{\int P dx}$ resolves the linear derivative form into a standard product-rule differential.

Step 1: Write down the standard form
We are given the linear differential equation representation:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x) y = Q(x) \quad \text{--- (Eq. A)}$$

Step 2: Introduce an unknown multiplier function $\mu(x)$
Multiply Eq. A on both sides by $\mu(x)$ hoping to form the derivative of a product on the left-hand side:
$$\mu(x) \frac{dy}{dx} + \mu(x) P(x) y = \mu(x) Q(x) \quad \text{--- (Eq. B)}$$

Step 3: Force the LHS to match Product Rule derivative of $(\mu(x) \cdot y)$
We know that $\frac{d}{dx}[\mu(x) \cdot y] = \mu(x)\frac{dy}{dx} + y\frac{d\mu}{dx}$. Comparing this to Eq. B, we require:
$$y \frac{d\mu}{dx} = \mu(x) P(x) y \implies \frac{d\mu}{dx} = \mu(x) P(x)$$

Step 4: Solve for $\mu(x)$ using Separation of Variables
Division by $\mu(x)$ and integration of both sides with respect to $x$ yields:
$$\int \frac{1}{\mu} d\mu = \int P(x) dx \implies \ln(\mu) = \int P(x) dx$$
Exponentiating both sides results in our definition of the Integrating Factor ($I.F.$):
$$\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) dx}$$

Step 5: Write the generalized complete solution
Once multiplied, Equation B can represent:
$$\frac{d}{dx}[y \cdot (I.F.)] = Q(x) \cdot (I.F.)$$
Simple integration of both sides with respect to $x$ delivers the final boxed answer structure:
$$y \cdot (I.F.) = \int Q(x) \cdot (I.F.) dx + C$$

JNTUK 8 Marks Derivation 02

Necessary and Sufficient Condition for Exactness ($Mdx + Ndy = 0$)

Proof that the partial derivatives matching $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$ is the sole mathematical requirement for exact differential solutions.

Part I: Proving Necessity (If the equation is exact, then partial derivatives are equal)
Suppose $Mdx + Ndy = 0$ is exact. This implies there exists a continuous function $u(x,y)$ such that:
$$du = M dx + N dy \quad \text{--- (Eq. C)}$$
But the definition of the mathematical total differential of $u(x,y)$ is:
$$du = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} dy \quad \text{--- (Eq. D)}$$
Comparing the coefficients of $dx$ and $dy$ in Eq. C and Eq. D, we must have:
$$M = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \quad \text{and} \quad N = \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}$$

Differentiate $M$ with respect to $y$, and $N$ with respect to $x$:
$$\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y \partial x} \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x \partial y}$$

By Clairaut's theorem on mixed partial derivatives, since $u$ and its partial indices are continuous:
$$\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y \partial x} = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x \partial y} \implies \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$$
This completes the proof of **necessity**.

🧮 5. Worked JNTUK Numerical Lab

Step-by-step math solver models
Problem 1: Linear 10 Marks High-Probability Model

Solve: $$(1+x^2)\frac{dy}{dx} + 2xy = \cos x$$

Solution:
Step 1: Convert to Standard Linear Form by dividing both sides through by $(1+x^2)$:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} + \left( \frac{2x}{1+x^2} \right) y = \frac{\cos x}{1+x^2}$$
This matches the standard form $\frac{dy}{dx} + P y = Q$, where:
$$P(x) = \frac{2x}{1+x^2} \quad \text{and} \quad Q(x) = \frac{\cos x}{1+x^2}$$

Step 2: Calculate the Integrating Factor (I.F.)
$$\int P dx = \int \frac{2x}{1+x^2} dx = \ln(1+x^2)$$
Therefore: $$I.F. = e^{\int P dx} = e^{\ln(1+x^2)} = 1+x^2$$

Step 3: Write down and integrate General Solution
$$y \cdot (I.F.) = \int Q(x) \cdot (I.F.) dx + C$$
Substituting our values:
$$y \cdot (1+x^2) = \int \left(\frac{\cos x}{1+x^2}\right) \cdot (1+x^2) dx + C$$
$$y \cdot (1+x^2) = \int \cos x dx + C$$
$$y \cdot (1+x^2) = \sin x + C \implies y = \frac{\sin x + C}{1+x^2}$$

Problem 2: Bernoulli 10 Marks Model

Solve: $$\frac{dy}{dx} + y \tan x = y^3 \sec x$$

Solution:
This equation matches the Bernoulli form $\frac{dy}{dx} + Py = Q y^n$ with $n = 3$.
Step 1: Divide through by $y^3$
$$y^{-3} \frac{dy}{dx} + y^{-2} \tan x = \sec x \quad \text{--- (Eq. 1)}$$

Step 2: Substitute $v = y^{1-n} = y^{-2}$
Differentiating both sides with respect to $x$:
$$\frac{dv}{dx} = -2 y^{-3} \frac{dy}{dx} \implies y^{-3} \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1}{2} \frac{dv}{dx}$$

Step 3: Convert Eq. 1 with substitutes
$$-\frac{1}{2} \frac{dv}{dx} + v \tan x = \sec x \implies \frac{dv}{dx} - 2v \tan x = -2 \sec x$$
This is now linear in $v$ with $P = -2\tan x$ and $Q = -2\sec x$.

Step 4: Compute Integrating Factor (I.F.)
$$\int P dx = -2 \int \tan x dx = -2 \ln(\sec x) = \ln(\sec^{-2} x) = \ln(\cos^2 x)$$
$$I.F. = e^{\ln(\cos^2 x)} = \cos^2 x$$

Step 5: General Solution
$$v \cdot I.F. = \int Q \cdot I.F. dx + C$$
$$v \cos^2 x = \int (-2 \sec x) \cos^2 x dx + C = -2 \int \cos x dx + C = -2\sin x + C$$
Replace $v = y^{-2}$:
$$\frac{1}{y^2} \cos^2 x = -2\sin x + C$$

Problem 3: Reducible to Exact 10 Marks Model

Solve: $$(x y^2 + 2x^2 y^3) dx + (x^2 y - x^3 y^2) dy = 0$$

Solution:
Here $M = xy^2 + 2x^2y^3$, so $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = 2xy + 6x^2y^2$.
And $N = x^2y - x^3y^2$, so $\frac{\partial N}{\partial x} = 2xy - 3x^2y^2$.
Since $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} \neq \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$, the equation is **not exact**.

Let's check if we can write $M = y(xy + 2x^2y^2)$ and $N = x(xy - x^2y^2)$. This fits the form $y f(xy)dx + x g(xy)dy = 0$.
Thus our Integrating Factor Rule 2 applies: $I.F. = \frac{1}{Mx - Ny}$.
Let's calculate the denominator:
$$Mx - Ny = x(xy^2 + 2x^2y^3) - y(x^2y - x^3y^2) = (x^2y^2 + 2x^3y^3) - (x^2y^2 - x^3y^3) = 3x^3y^3$$
So $I.F. = \frac{1}{3x^3y^3}$ (we can drop the factor $3$ and use $I.F. = \frac{1}{x^3y^3}$).

Multiply the original equation by $\frac{1}{x^3y^3}$:
$$\left( \frac{xy^2}{x^3y^3} + \frac{2x^2y^3}{x^3y^3} \right) dx + \left( \frac{x^2y}{x^3y^3} - \frac{x^3y^2}{x^3y^3} \right) dy = 0$$
$$\left( \frac{1}{x^2 y} + \frac{2}{x} \right) dx + \left( \frac{1}{x y^2} - \frac{1}{y} \right) dy = 0$$
Let's verify new exactness: $M' = \frac{1}{x^2y} + \frac{2}{x} \implies \frac{\partial M'}{\partial y} = -\frac{1}{x^2y^2}$.
$N' = \frac{1}{xy^2} - \frac{1}{y} \implies \frac{\partial N'}{\partial x} = -\frac{1}{x^2y^2}$. It matches! It is exact.

Complete Integration:
$$\int M' dx = \int \left( x^{-2}y^{-1} + \frac{2}{x} \right) dx = -\frac{1}{xy} + 2\ln|x|$$
$$\int (\text{terms of } N' \text{ independent of } x) dy = \int -\frac{1}{y} dy = -\ln|y|$$
$$\text{Solution: } -\frac{1}{xy} + 2\ln|x| - \ln|y| = C$$

Problem 4: Newton's Cooling 10 Marks Application Model

Solve: A copper ball is heated to $100^\circ\text{C}$. At $t = 0$ it is placed in water maintained at $30^\circ\text{C}$. After 3 minutes, the ball's temperature is $70^\circ\text{C}$. Find its temperature at $t = 15$ minutes.

Solution:
Here surrounding constant temp $T_s = 30^\circ\text{C}$ and initial temp $T_0 = 100^\circ\text{C}$.
Standard Newton equation:
$$T(t) = T_s + (T_0 - T_s) e^{-kt} = 30 + (100 - 30)e^{-kt} = 30 + 70e^{-kt}$$

Step 1: Calculate the damping constant $k$ using condition at $t = 3$ min ($T = 70^\circ\text{C}$)
$$70 = 30 + 70e^{-3k} \implies 40 = 70e^{-3k} \implies e^{-3k} = \frac{4}{7} \approx 0.5714$$
$$-3k = \ln(0.5714) = -0.5596 \implies k \approx 0.1865 \text{ min}^{-1}$$

Step 2: Calculate temperature at $t = 15$ min
$$e^{-15k} = (e^{-3k})^5 = (4/7)^5 \approx 0.0612$$
Therefore:
$$T(15) = 30 + 70 e^{-15k} = 30 + 70(0.0612) = 30 + 4.28 = 34.28^\circ\text{C}$$
$$\text{Answer: The temperature after 15 minutes is } 34.28^\circ\text{C}$$

Q1. What is the fundamental difference between standard Linear and Bernoulli's ODEs?

Tap to flip answer 🔄

Linear DE has power $1$ on $y$ and $dy/dx$, and they are never multiplied. Bernoulli's DE has a factor $y^n$ multiplying the right hand function $Q(x)$, which breaks linearity.

Matched Examiner Expectation ✅

Q2. How is a non-exact equation transformed into an exact differential equation?

Tap to flip answer 🔄

By multiplying the entire equation by a scale factor called the Integrating Factor (I.F.), which satisfies the condition $\partial(M \cdot I.F.)/\partial y = \partial(N \cdot I.F.)/\partial x$.

Matched Examiner Expectation ✅

Topper Shortcuts: Integrating Factor Checks

Always evaluate coordinates before integrating:

• Homogeneous equations mean the sum of powers of variables in every term is identical. Keep eyes on exponents!

• If $M = y e^x y \cos y$, homogeneous checks yield false. Instantly proceed to rule 3 or rule 4.

01

Introduction: The Nature of Wave Optics

Physical optics or Wave Optics is the branch of physics that studies properties of light where the ray approximation of geometrical optics is invalid (specifically when wave interactions occur around obstacles sized closely to the wavelength of light, $\lambda \approx 10^{-7}\text{ m}$).

The Core Crux of R23: The entire unit revolves around three phase-driven light field phenomena: Interference (division of amplitude or wavefront), Diffraction (bending of waves around corners), and Polarization (restricting electromagnetic oscillations to a single plane).

Coherence Demystified

For sustained interference, waves must be Coherent (maintain a constant relative phase angle $\Delta\phi$ with time).

Spatial Coherence Constant phase across the transverse wavefront profile. Test tool: Young's Slits.
Temporal Coherence Constant phase along the direction of wave travel. Test tool: Michelson path checks.

Key Concepts of Interference & Superposition

When two light waves of frequency $f$ and initial phase angle traverse the medium and overlaps, the instantaneous algebraic sum of the fields defines the physical state.

Fundamental Relationship

Phase Difference ($\phi$) vs Path Difference ($\Delta$)

φ = (π / λ) * Δ

This conversion is critical for every single derivation in JNTUK R23.

Resultant Intensity

Mathematical General Equation

I = I₁ + I₂ + 2 √(I₁ I₂) cos(φ)

If amplitudes match ($a_1 = a_2 = a$), maximum intensity is $4a^2$ (constructive) and minimum is $0$ (destructive).

Physical Property
Constructive Interference (Bright)
Destructive Interference (Dark)
Path Difference (Δ)
Δ = nλ
Δ = (2n + 1)λ / 2
Phase Difference (φ)
φ = 2nπ
φ = (2n + 1)π
Fringe Intensity
I_max ∝ (a₁ + a₂)²
I_min ∝ (a₁ - a₂)²

Important Definitions with Exam Keyword Focus

Write these definitions word-for-word in the examination to claim full credit from paper testers:

01. Coherent Sources

Two sources are defined as coherent if they emit light waves of identical wavelength ($\lambda$), identical frequency ($f$), and which maintain a constant or zero initial phase difference over time.

02. division of amplitude

The method of dividing the amplitude of a single incoming ray of light into two or more parts using reflection or refraction mechanisms. Examples: Newton’s Rings, Wedge-shaped films.

03. Stokes' Treatment

An electromagnetic physical concept stating that when a light wave reflections from the surface of an optically denser medium, it undergoes an abrupt phase change of π (equivalent to path correction of $\lambda/2$).

04. Resolving Power (Grating)

The ability of a diffraction grating to separate two closely aligned wavelengths of spectral lines. Calculated mathematically as: R = λ / dλ = nN.

05. Brewster’s Law

States that the tangent of the polarizing angle of incidence ($i_p$) is numerically equal to the refractive index ($\mu$) of the refracting medium: tan(i_p) = μ. No reflection components are unpolarized at this state.

06. Uniaxial Crystals

Crystals containing one axis of optical symmetry along which the Ordinary (O-ray) and Extraordinary (E-ray) waves travel with identical velocity. Examples: Calcite, Quartz.

Important Derivations — Step-by-Step Step-by-Step Model Answers

JNTUK 10-Marks Target Derivation

1. Concentric Ring Diameter Derivation (Newton's Rings Setup)

Physical Setup: A plano-convex lens of big radius of curvature $R$ is placed over a flat optical glass plate, trapping a wedge of air of variable thickness $t$ extending from the point of contact.

Mathematical Chain:

1

The Wedge Path Difference:

Path difference between rays reflected from upper and lower interface of air film is:

Δ = 2μt cos(r) + λ/2

For normal incidence ($r=0$) and air film ($\mu=1$): Δ = 2t + λ/2. (Stokes' correction is the source of the $+\lambda/2$ term).

2

Enforcing Dark Fringes Condition:

For destructive interference (minimum intensity: dark rings):

2t + λ/2 = (2n + 1)λ/2   →   2t = nλ (where n = 0, 1, 2, 3...)

3

Geometry of Plano-Convex Lens:

By the theorem of intersecting chords (or lens circle geometry equations):

r² = 2Rt - t²

Since thickness $t$ is tiny relative to curvature radius $R$ ($t$ << $R$), neglecting $t^2$ yields:

t = r² / 2R = Dₙ² / 8R   (Substitute radius of ring $r$ as diameter $D_n/2$)

✓

Final Dark Ring Diameter Equation:

Injecting step 3 thickness back into destructive step 2:

Dₙ² = 4nλR   →   Dₙ = 2 √(nλR)

This proves the Dark Ring Diameter $D_n$ is proportional to the square root of natural integers ($D_n \propto \sqrt{n}$).

5

Bright Ring Diameter Equation:

Similarly, enforcing constructive condition $2t + \lambda/2 = n\lambda$ (where $\Delta = n\lambda$):

Dₙ² = 2(2n - 1)λR

This proves Bright Ring Diameter $D_n$ is proportional to the square root of odd natural numbers ($D_n \propto \sqrt{2n-1}$).

High Yield Derivation

2. Wedge-Shaped Thin Film Fringe path difference

Setup: Two planar glass sheets inclined at a super flat wedge angle $\theta$ touch at one end. Light of wavelength $\lambda$ is incident upon it.

The path difference equation at location of thickness $t$:

Δ = 2μt cos(r + θ) + λ/2

Due to the constant wedge spacing gradient ($t = x\tan\theta \approx x\theta$), fringes generated are parallel lines of constant flat thickness. The spacing between consecutive bright bands (Fringe Width $\beta$) is calculated as:

β = λ / (2 μ θ)

👉 Board Trap Warning: Do not forget to state $\theta$ is in Radians, not Degrees!

Fraunhofer Derivation

3. Diffraction Grating Governing Equation

A diffraction grating consists of N parallel equivalent slits separated by opaque spaces of size $b$. Let single slit width be $a$.

1. The Grating element constant is determined as: (a + b).

2. Reinforcing path diff condition for maxima (diffracted spectral peaks):

(a + b) sin(θ) = nλ

3. If the grid contains $N'$ grooves or lines per unit length (e.g. lines per inch), then:

(a + b) = 1 / N'

High-Precision Formula Sheet

Newton's Rings Wavelength

Determining Liquid Refractive Index μ

μ = [ Dₙ₊ₘ² - Dₙ² ]_air / [ Dₙ₊ₘ² - Dₙ² ]_liquid

Useful when finding chemical properties utilizing optical diffraction experiments.

Diffraction Spectrometer

Resolving Power R and Dispersive Power dθ/dλ

R = nN   |   dθ/dλ = n / [ (a+b) cos(θ) ]

N: Total active lines of grating exposed to incident wavefront.

Wave Polarization

Malus Law & Brewster’s angle relationship

I = I_0 cos²(θ)   |   μ = tan(i_p)

At Brewster angle $i_p$, the reflected ray and refracted ray are exactly $90^\circ$ perpendicular.

Polarizer Plates

Quarter (λ/4) & Half Wave (λ/2) Plate Thickness

t_QWP = λ / [ 4(|μ_o - μ_e|) ]
t_HWP = λ / [ 2(|μ_o - μ_e|) ]

μ_o, μ_e: Refractive indexes of ordinary and extraordinary wave channels.

Solved Numerical Problems (R23 Repeated)

Newton’s Rings Standard

Problem 1: Diameter of the 10th Dark Ring Calculation

In a Newton's rings experiment, the diameter of the 10th dark ring is $0.4\text{ cm}$. Find the wavelength of the light source used if curvature radius $R = 1\text{ m}$.

Solution:

1. Identify Given Data:

n = 10 (order index)
D_10 = 0.4 cm = 0.4 * 10^-2 m
R = 1 m (radius of plano lens)

2. Formula applied for dark rings:

Dₙ² = 4nλR   →   λ = Dₙ² / (4nR)

3. Calculate:

λ = (0.4 * 10^-2)² / (4 * 10 * 1)
λ = (1.6 * 10^-5) / 40 = 4.0 * 10^-7 m = 4000 Å

⚠️ Topper’s Tip: Always display wavelength in Angstroms (Å) or Nanometers (nm). 1 Å = 10^-10 m, 1 nm = 10^-9 m.

Wedge Film Standard

Problem 2: Wedge Angle determination for Fringe Separation

A wedge-shaped air film receives monochromatic light of wavelength $589\text{ nm}$. The distance between 10 consecutive bright fringes is $1.2\text{ cm}$. Find the angle of the wedge.

Solution:

1. Identify Given Data:

λ = 589 nm = 589 * 10^-9 m
Total width of 10 fringes = 1.2 cm = 1.2 * 10^-2 m
Fringe spacing β = Total distance / 10 = 1.2 * 10^-3 m

2. Formula applied for wedge spacing:

β = λ / 2θ   →   θ = λ / 2β   (For air μ=1)

3. Calculate:

θ = (589 * 10^-9) / (2 * 1.2 * 10^-3)
θ = 2.45 * 10^-4 radians (which is 0.014 degrees)

JNTUK Board Solved PYQs

JNTUK Nov 2024 Exam

Why is the central ring dark in Newton's rings?

5 Marks

Explain why the central spot in Newton's interference rings formed by reflected light is dark.

Bullet Grading Checklist:

1. At the point of contact between the plano-convex lens and optical glass plate, the film thickness $t$ is exactly zero ($t = 0$).

2. The reflection optical path difference equation under normal reflection context is: Δ = 2μt cos(r) + λ/2.

3. Injecting $t=0$: Δ = λ/2.

4. Since the path difference is exactly half a wavelength, it satisfies the strict phase condition of destructive interference.

5. Thus, the center of the ring pattern is consistently dark in reflected patterns.

JNTUK April 2024 Exam

What is Resolving Power of diffraction grating?

7 Marks

Define resolving power. Derive the expression for the resolving power of a planar transmission grating spectrum.

Derivation Milestones:

1. Definition: The capability of separating spectral lines of extremely matching wavelengths. $R = \lambda / d\lambda$.

2. Apply Rayleigh's Criterion: First diffraction minimum of wavelength $\lambda + d\lambda$ falls exactly over principal diffraction maximum of wavelength $\lambda$.

3. Equation for principal maximum for $\lambda$ at angle $\theta$: (a+b) sinθ = nλ.

4. Equation for adjacent minimum of $\lambda+d\lambda$ at nearby angle: N(a+b) sin(θ+dθ) = Nn(λ+dλ).

5. Solving both systems yields final target equation: R = λ/dλ = nN (where $n$ is diffraction order, $N$ is lines illuminated).

Quick Revision Sheets

1. Interference Types

• Division of Wavefront: Single wave split spatially. Slit apertures required (e.g. Young's DS, Diffraction Grating).
• Division of Amplitude: Incident path subdivided by partial mirror surfaces. Flat wavefront preserved (e.g. Newton’s rings).

2. Diffraction Grating

• Peak maximum condition: $(a+b)\sin\theta = n\lambda$.
• If incident beam normal: maximum observable order $n_{max} \le (a+b)/\lambda$.
• Absent spectra occur when single slit minimum overlaps grating maximum: $n/m = (a+b)/a$.

3. Optical Plates

• QWP thickness: $\lambda / 4(\mu_o - \mu_e)$. Creates circular polarization when linearly plane components exit.
• HWP thickness: $\lambda / 2(\mu_o - \mu_e)$. Rotates incoming plane angle by $2\theta$.

Memory Tricks & Mnemonics

Trick A: Newton’s Rings Conditions (C.O.P)

To recall how Stokes path affects boundaries:

C - Center point is dark because thickness value is zero.
O - Optical reflection boundary is rarer-versus-denser.
P - Phase addition of exactly $\pi$ ($\lambda/2$ path) occurs.

Trick B: Uniaxial polarization index (C-A-L)

To recall Negative crystal parameters:

C - Calcite has ordinary index larger than extraordinary index.
A - Active Extraordinary Wave travels faster than Ordinary Wave.
L - Low extraordinary index is why calcite is classified negative.

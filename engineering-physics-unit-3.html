1. Unit Overview & Exam Weight

This unit splits mathematically and qualitatively into two major halves: Dielectrics (insulators acting under extreme capacitive fields) and Magnetic properties of solids. JNTUK extremely heavily tests the physical representation of dielectric constant, polarization, and mathematical formulation of Clausius-Mossotti and B-H loops.

Exam Importance

Both dielectric and magnetic sub-questions appear as major 10M selections in external papers. The Clausius-Mossotti proof and B-H loops are prime repeats.

Practical Application

High-permittivity dielectrics are core to modern microchip capacitors whereas magnetic materials drive transformers and storage memories.

2. Complete Syllabus Core Concepts

Concept 01

Dielectric Constant & Susceptibility

Definition: Dielectric constant ($\varepsilon_r$) is the ratio of permittivity of the medium to permittivity of free space. Polarization vector $P$ is proportional to E-field via electric susceptibility ($\chi_e$).

P = \varepsilon_0 \chi_e E
Concept 02

4 Polarizability Components

Induced dipole formation occurs via four mechanisms:

  • Electronic: Displacement of electron cloud relative to nucleus.
  • Ionic: Separation of positive/negative ions.
  • Orientation: Alignment of pre-existing polar molecules.
  • Space Charge: Accumulation of mobile carriers at boundaries.
Concept 03

Lorentz Internal Field (E_local)

Definition: The actual electric field acting on a single specific atom tucked inside a dielectric bulk. Sum of external field ($E$) and space-field induced by surrounding dipoles.

E_local = E + P / (3 * \varepsilon_0)

Common Trap: E_local is always higher than raw average macroscopic field E in isotropic cubic crystals!

Concept 04

Clausius-Mossotti Equation

Definition: Relates macroscopic dielectric constant ($\varepsilon_r$) to microscopic polarizability ($\alpha_e$). Directly models how single-atom properties yield bulk materials values.

(\varepsilon_r - 1) / (\varepsilon_r + 2) = (N \alpha_e) / (3 \varepsilon_0)
Concept 05

Magnetic Domains & Walls

Definition: Microscopic regions inside ferromagnetic solids where all magnetic moments are spontaneously aligned in the same direction. Separated by transitioning "domain walls" whose displacement models saturation.

Magnetization occurs through domain wall displacement (reversible/irreversible) and domain rotation under strong external fields.

Concept 06

Soft vs Hard Magnets

Soft Magnets: Easily magnetized and demagnetized. Low coercivity, thin Hysteresis loop, low power dissipation (losses). Used in AC transformers.

Hard Magnets: High magnetic retentivity and exceptionally high coercivity. Extremely wide loop. Used to fabricate stable permanent magnets.

Concept 07

Displacement & Relations between Vectors

Definition: Relates the applied electric field $E$, polarization density of the material $P$, and dielectric displacement vector $D$. This is a major textbook question.

D = \varepsilon_0 E + P = \varepsilon_0 \varepsilon_r E

Shortcut: Useful for calculating displacement density in micro-strip capacitor problems.

Concept 08

Atomic Origin of Magnetism & Bohr Magneton

Definition: Magnetism arises from two motions of atomic electrons: (1) orbital rotation of electron around nucleus, (2) spin of the electron on its own axis. The smallest unit is the Bohr Magneton ($\mu_B$).

\mu_B = \frac{e\hbar}{2m} = 9.274 \times 10^{-24} \text{ A}\cdot\text{m}^2
Concept 09

Magnetic Vector Relations

Definition: In any magnetic medium, the net magnetic induction vector $B$ is a superposition of the magnetizing field $H$ and the induced bulk magnetization $M$.

B = \mu_0(H + M) = \mu_0 \mu_r H \quad \text{and} \quad \chi_m = M / H

Formula Tip: Relative permeability is relates to susceptibility via: $\mu_r = 1 + \chi_m$.

Concept 10

Hysteresis Loop Metrics

Definition: The lagging of magnetic induction $B$ behind the magnetizing field $H$ during cyclic magnetization. Important parameters:

  • Retentivity ($B_r$): The remnant induction left when $H$ is dropped to zero.
  • Coercivity ($H_c$): The demagnetizing force required to drop $B$ to zero.
  • Energy Loss: Directly proportional to the area enclosed by the loop.
03

Types & Frequency Dependence of Polarizations

Crystalline lattices polarize differently under electric fields across different operating frequencies. As the frequency response increases, heavier molecular mechanisms fail to align, eventually leaving only electronic vibrations.

Electronic (Quantitative)

Electronic Polarizability

Displacement of electron clouds centered at nucleus. Independent of temperature. f ≤ 10¹⁵ Hz (Optical range).

Ionic (Quantitative)

Ionic Polarizability

Relative displacement of positive and negative ion centers. Independent of temperature. f ≤ 10¹³ Hz (Infrared range).

Orientation (Qualitative)

Orientational (Dipolar)

Alignment of polar molecules with permanent dipole moments. Highly temperature dependent ($\propto 1/T$). f ≤ 10¹⁰ Hz (Microwave/Radio).

Space Charge

Space Charge Accumulation

Migration of charges to interfaces of different phases. Extremely slow reaction rate. f ≤ 10³ Hz (Power line frequency).

Complex Polarization Limits

Frequency Dependence & Dielectric Loss

When a fluctuating AC field $\mathbf{E} = E_0 e^{i\omega t}$ is loaded, polar orientation flags a phase lag. Permittivity shifts into a math expression complex dielectric constant:

ϵ* = ϵ' - i ϵ''

Where ϵ' is the real permittivity (storage), and ϵ'' is the imaginary permittivity (loss component reflecting dissipation). The dissipation tangent is:

tan(δ) = ϵ'' / ϵ' ⇒ Loss = 2π f Ε₀ ϵ'' E²

Note: Dielectric loss is proportional to power thermal losses inside capacitor cores operating under high AC frequencies.

High-Yield Formula Sheet & Core Constants

1. Polarization Vector Relationship

P = N α E_local
P = ε_0 (ε_r - 1) E

N is number density, alpha is the microscopic atomic susceptibility parameter.

2. Displacement Vector (D)

D = ε_0 E + P = ε_0 ε_r E

This connects the macroscopic displacement field to the bulk polarization state.

3. Clausius-Mossotti Equation

(ε_r - 1) / (ε_r + 2) = N α / (3 ε_0)

Applicable strictly to elemental solids with high isotropic symmetric properties inside 3D cubic lattices.

4. Magnetic Induction & Susceptibility

B = μ_0 (H + M)
χ_m = M / H
μ_r = 1 + χ_m

H: field intensity; M: bulk magnetization vector; B: magnetic induction; μ_r: relative permeability.

Step-by-Step Board Derivations

JNTUK Prime Repeat (7-10 Marks)

1. Clausius-Mossotti Equation Derivation

Goal: Mathematically establish bulk dielectric constant ε_r relative to individual atomic polarizability parameters.

Step 1: Express Polarization (P) using Local Lorentz and Macroscopic fields
Bulk Polarization is proportional to localized field: $P = N \alpha E_{\text{local}} \quad \text{--- (Eq. 1)}$
Also, from electrostatic boundary equations: $P = \varepsilon_0 (\varepsilon_r - 1) E \quad \text{--- (Eq. 2)}$

Step 2: Introduce Lorentz Local field & substitute
Lorentz internal local field inside a symmetric cubic lattice: $E_{\text{local}} = E + \frac{P}{3\varepsilon_0}$
Substitute this into Equation 1:
$P = N \alpha \left( E + \frac{P}{3\varepsilon_0} \right) \implies P = N \alpha E + \frac{N \alpha P}{3\varepsilon_0}$
$P \left( 1 - \frac{N \alpha}{3\varepsilon_0} \right) = N \alpha E \implies E = \frac{P}{N \alpha} \left( 1 - \frac{N \alpha}{3\varepsilon_0} \right) \quad \text{--- (Eq. 3)}$

Step 3: Combine equations to eliminate E
From Equation 2, the macroscopic field is: $E = \frac{P}{\varepsilon_0(\varepsilon_r - 1)}$. Equating this to Equation 3:
$\frac{P}{\varepsilon_0(\varepsilon_r - 1)} = \frac{P}{N\alpha} \left(1 - \frac{N\alpha}{3\varepsilon_0}\right)$
Divide both sides by $P$ and rearrange terms:
$\frac{N\alpha}{\varepsilon_0(\varepsilon_r - 1)} = 1 - \frac{N\alpha}{3\varepsilon_0} \implies \frac{N\alpha}{\varepsilon_0} \left[ \frac{1}{\varepsilon_r - 1} + \frac{1}{3} \right] = 1$
$\frac{N\alpha}{\varepsilon_0} \left[ \frac{3 + \varepsilon_r - 1}{3(\varepsilon_r - 1)} \right] = 1 \implies \frac{N\alpha}{3\varepsilon_0} \left[ \frac{\varepsilon_r + 2}{\varepsilon_r - 1} \right] = 1$
Rearranging to isolate macroscopic and microscopic parameters:
\frac{\varepsilon_r - 1}{\varepsilon_r + 2} = \frac{N \alpha}{3 \varepsilon_0} This is the famous **Clausius-Mossotti relation**, fundamental to solid state physical optics.

⚠️ Common Error: Do not confuse $E$ (macroscopic field) with $E_{\text{local}}$ (local field felt by an atom). Mixing them up will cost you full marks.
定量推导 QUANTITATIVE DERIVATION

2. Electronic Polarizability ($\alpha_e$) Derivation

Goal: Formulate the electric displacement of the electron cloud to solve electronic polarizability volume dependency.

Step 1: Model the Atom
Consider a classical nuclear model with atomic charge $+Ze$ centered inside a spherical negative electron cloud of radius $R$ with total negative charge $-Ze$ distributed uniformly.

Step 2: Apply External Electric Field $E$
Under an applied field $E$, the positive nucleus shifts in the direction of the field, and the electron cloud shifts in the opposite direction. Let $d$ be the equilibrium separation distance between their centers.

Step 3: Equate Electrostatic and Restoring Forces
At equilibrium, the electrostatic restoring force pulling the electron cloud back equals the force exerted by the external field:
• **Force of external field:** $F_{\text{field}} = Z e E$
• **Electrostatic restoring force:** By Gauss's Law, the charge contained inside a sphere of radius $d$ is $q' = Ze \left(\frac{d}{R}\right)^3$. The Coulomb force between this fraction of charge and the nucleus is:
$F_{\text{restore}} = \frac{(Ze) q'}{4 \pi \varepsilon_0 d^2} = \frac{(Ze) (Ze \frac{d^3}{R^3})}{4 \pi \varepsilon_0 d^2} = \frac{Z^2 e^2 d}{4 \pi \varepsilon_0 R^3}$
Equating $F_{\text{field}} = F_{\text{restore}}$ at steady equilibrium state:
$Z e E = \frac{Z^2 e^2 d}{4 \pi \varepsilon_0 R^3} \implies d = \frac{4 \pi \varepsilon_0 R^3}{Z e} E$

Step 4: Compute Induced Dipole Moment & Polarizability
The induced electronic dipole moment is defined as:
$\mu_{\text{ind}} = Z e d = Z e \left( \frac{4 \pi \varepsilon_0 R^3}{Z e} E \right) = 4 \pi \varepsilon_0 R^3 E$
Since the induced dipole moment is also expressed as $\mu_{\text{ind}} = \alpha_e E$, we get:
\alpha_e = 4 \pi \varepsilon_0 R^3 Electronic polarizability is directly proportional to the volume of the atom ($V = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3$). This proves why larger atoms have higher electronic polarizability values!

3. Ionic Polarizability ($\alpha_i$) Derivation

Goal: Formulate polarizability due to displacement of positive and negative ions in a diatomic crystal.

Step 1: Define Crystalline Ions Parameters
Consider a diatomic ionic solid. Let $M$ be the mass of positive ion (e.g. $\text{Na}^+$) and $m$ be the mass of negative ion (e.g. $\text{Cl}^-$). Both carry equal and opposite charge $e$.

Step 2: Apply Electric Field to Shift Centers
Under field $E$, positive ions shift by distance $x_1$ and negative ions shift by $x_2$ in opposite directions. The restoring force constant is given by $k = \omega_0^2 \mu$ (where $\omega_0$ is the angular frequency of lattice vibration). At equilibrium:
• **For positive ion:** $e E = M \omega_0^2 x_1 \implies x_1 = \frac{e E}{M \omega_0^2}$
• **For negative ion:** $e E = m \omega_0^2 x_2 \implies x_2 = \frac{e E}{m \omega_0^2}$

Step 3: Total Displacement & Dipole Moment
The total displacement separating positive and negative centers of mass is:
$x = x_1 + x_2 = \frac{e E}{\omega_0^2} \left[ \frac{1}{M} + \frac{1}{m} \right]$
The corresponding induced ionic dipole moment is:
$\mu_i = e x = \frac{e^2 E}{\omega_0^2} \left[ \frac{1}{M} + \frac{1}{m} \right]$
Since $\mu_i = \alpha_i E$, ionic polarizability is isolated as:
\alpha_i = \frac{e^2}{\omega_0^2} \left[ \frac{1}{M} + \frac{1}{m} \right] This demonstrates how ionic polarizability varies inversely with the square of the lattice resonant mechanical frequency.

5. Fully Solved JNTUK Numerical Models

Problem Model 1

Problem: A dielectric material has an electric susceptibility of $4.5$ at room temperature. Determine the relative permittivity (ϵ_r) of the material and the absolute bulk polarization vector $P$ under a macroscopic field of $3 \times 10^4\text{ V/m}$.

Solution steps:
1. Relationship: $\varepsilon_r = 1 + \chi_e = 1 + 4.5 = 5.5$.
2. Polarization: $P = \varepsilon_0 \chi_e E = (8.854 \times 10^{-12}\text{ F/m}) \times 4.5 \times (3 \times 10^4\text{ V/m}) = 1.195 \times 10^{-6}\text{ C/m}^2$.
Final Outputs: Dielectric constant = 5.5, Polarization P = 1.195e-6 C/m²

JNTUK Solved Papers

JNTUK Nov 2024 Exam Paper 10 Marks Selection

Question: Explain Lorentz internal local field inside a symmetric cubic dielectric. Obtain the formula.

Full Score Answer Steps:

  1. Introduction: Define $E_{\text{local}}$ as the actual localized field acting on an atom wrapped inside dielectric materials bulk.
  2. Field Divisions: Define the concept of the imaginary Lorentz cavity sphere of radius $R$. Divide the total local field into 4 core components:
    $E_{\text{local}} = E_1 + E_2 + E_3 + E_4$ • $E_1$: Field due to external charged plates.
    • $E_2$: Depolarizing field from surface charges ($E_2 = -P/\varepsilon_0$).
    • $E_3$: Field due to polarization charges on the cavity surface ($E_3 = P/3\varepsilon_0$).
    • $E_4$: Field due to dipoles residing within the cavity (cancels out to zero in symmetric cubic cells).
  3. Final Formula: Combining macroscopic field $E = E_1 + E_2$, we yield:
    $E_{\text{local}} = E + \frac{P}{3\varepsilon_0}$
JNTUK May 2023 Exam Paper 10 Marks Selection

Question: State the differences between soft and hard magnetic materials. Sketch their hysteresis loops.

Core Comparative Points to write:

  • Coercivity: Soft magnets have very low coercivity ($H_c < 100 \text{ A/m}$); hard magnets have very high coercivity ($H_c > 10^4 \text{ A/m}$).
  • Retentivity: Soft magnets have high retentivity but demagnetize easily; hard magnets retain alignment permanently.
  • Area of Loop (Hysteresis Loss): Soft magnets have a narrow, thin loop (low core heating losses); hard magnets have a very broad loop (high energy store).
  • Applications: Soft magnets are used inside AC transformers and electromagnets; hard magnets are used for permanent magnets in DC motors.

Q1. What is the effect of temperature on electronic and ionic polarizabilities?

Flip card to read answer 🔄

Both electronic and ionic polarizabilities are entirely independent of temperature. Temperature only alters orientation properties parameters because thermal agitation opposes alignment!

Q2. Define retentivity and coercivity of magnetic cores?

Flip card to read answer 🔄

Retentivity is the residual magnetic induction ($B_r$) left in the core when external magnetic intensity $H$ drops to zero. Coercivity ($H_c$) is the reverse field required to reduce residual magnetization to zero.

Verified JNTUK Viva Answer

Q3. Why are ferrites excellent for high frequency transformer cores?

Flip card to read answer 🔄

Ferrites are ferrimagnetic oxides with exceptionally high electrical resistance. High resistivity cuts eddy current heating losses to near zero under high AC operating frequencies!

Verified JNTUK Viva Answer

Q4. What is the Clausius-Mossotti equation restriction?

Flip card to read answer 🔄

It is applicable only to highly symmetric, isotropic elemental solids (like cubic lattices) where there are no permanent dipolar moments under zero applied fields.

Quick Revision Sheets

DIELECTRIC VECTORS

• Permittivity: Capacity of core medium to polarize ($\varepsilon$).
• Susceptibility ($\chi_e$): Scale of polarization alignment vector per unit applied field force. $P = \varepsilon_0 \chi_e E$.
• Displacement ($D$): Combines applied field influence and medium polarization response. $D = \varepsilon_0 E + P$.

MAGNETIC SCALES

• Dipole Moment ($\mu_m$): Quantum spin & orbital electrons torque product.
• Magnetization ($M$): Net magnetic dipole moment induced per unit volume.
• Susceptibility ($\chi_m$): Ratio of induced magnetization $M$ to field $H$ ($\chi_m = M/H$).

HYSTERESIS METRICS

• Domain wall movement: Spontaneous boundary shifting occurring under low applied external fields.
• Domain rotation: Hard structural moment rotation matching strong direction fields.
• Hysteresis Area: Measures thermal losses occurring during cyclic magnetization.

Remembering Electric vs Magnetic Vectors

• DIELECTRICS (Electric) starts with "D" ⇒ uses Displacement Vector, Polarization, and electric Susceptibility ($\chi_e$).

$D = \varepsilon_0 E + P \quad \longleftrightarrow \quad B = \mu_0(H + M)$

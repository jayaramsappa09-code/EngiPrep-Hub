Unit 1

DC Circuit Analysis: Core Theorems

Let's demystify Network Theorems. You will always get one 14-Mark question (or split 7-Mark questions) from DC Circuit Theorems in your JNTUK External Exams. The two highest probability theorems are Thevenin's Theorem and Superposition Theorem.

Thevenin’s Theorem Simplified Steps

Any linear bilateral network containing active independent sources and resistances can be simplified into a single voltage source ($V_{th}$) in series with a single equivalent resistor ($R_{th}$).

Step 1: Open load $R_L$

Remove the load resistor across terminals $A$ & $B$. This leaves the terminal open-circuited.

Step 2: Calculate $V_{th}$

Determine the open-circuit voltage across terminals $A-B$ using standard Mesh, Nodal, or Loop analysis.

Step 3: Calculate $R_{th}$

Deactivate all sources (Voltage source → Short circuit, Current source → Open circuit) & look inside $A-B$.

📐 Formula for Load Current: I_L = V_th / (R_th + R_L)
Unit 2

AC Circuits & Resonance basics

Alternating quantities are defined as sinusoidal waves. The most crucial topics are RMS value derivation, average value calculation, and Series RLC Resonance.

RLC Series Resonance Derivation

Resonance in a series RLC circuit occurs when the active inductive reactance ($X_L$) offsets the capacitive reactance ($X_C$), causing the network impedance to become purely resistive.

At Resonance Condition:

X_L = X_C ==> 2 * π * f_r * L = 1 / (2 * π * f_r * C)

f_r^2 = 1 / (4 * π^2 * L * C)

f_r = 1 / (2 * π * √(L * C))

  • At resonance, Impedance $Z = R$ is at its minimum.
  • Current $I = V / R$ is at its maximum peak value.
  • Power factor is equal to unity ($\cos \phi = 1$).
Unit 3

DC Machines & Transformers

This unit holds massive weight because it contains core mechanical-to-electrical converter equations. Do not skip either the Transformer EMF Equation or DC Motor EMF derivation.

⚡ EMF Equation of a Transformer

Let $N_1$ be the number of primary turns, $N_2$ be the secondary turns, $f$ be the frequency, and $\Phi_m$ be the maximum value of magnetic flux inside the core.

// Average rate of change of flux:

dΦ/dt = e_avg = 4 * f * Φ_m

// Form Factor (sinusoidal) = RMS value / Average value = 1.11:

e_rms = 1.11 * e_avg = 4.44 * f * Φ_m

// RMS voltage inside winding:

E_1 = 4.44 * f * N_1 * Φ_m

E_2 = 4.44 * f * N_2 * Φ_m

⚡ Memory Tricks & Exam Shortcuts

The "SOU-VOLT" Theorem Rule

When applying Superposition, deactivate each source recursively: Voltage source gets shorted (0V represents a line element); Current source gets open-circuited (0A represents an air gap/disconnect).

Transformer Speed Trap

Always solve for efficiency using equivalent loads. Never multiply $kVA$ directly if power factors differ; calculate active winding power first via: $P = kVA \times \cos \phi$.

Circuit Laws

Ohm & KV Laws

Total voltage gains around any closed loop must equal sum of voltage drops ($\sum V = 0$). Currents entering node equal outputs ($\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}$).

AC Impedance

Impedance $Z$ Equation

Z = R + j(X_L - X_C)
|Z| = √(R² + (X_L - X_C)²)

❓ FAQs Students Search

How can I pass JNTUK BEEE easily on the last day? ▼

Focus strictly on Network Theorems (Thevenin's and Superposition) and Electrical Machine EMF equations. Completing these two topics perfectly gives you 24+ marks instantly, ensuring you clear the passing criteria line of 26 marks with zero friction.

What are copper losses and where do they occur in transformer coils? ▼

Copper losses are $I^2R$ power dissipations occurring due to the internal resistance of primary and secondary windings. These losses are dynamic and scale as the square of the load current ($P_{cu} \propto I^2$).
